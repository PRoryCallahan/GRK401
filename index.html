              Welcome to Introduction to Greek #

              This site is designed as a course aid and supplement for both GRK401: Introduction to Greek I and GRK402: Introduction to Greek II at St. Gregory the Great Seminary.

              Course Modules #

              Why Learn Greek? #

              Each may have his particular reasons for wanting to learn Greek. My own brief answer is that Greek is beautiful and the culture and thought unearthed by this language is strange and wonder-provoking. For the Catholic, to whom God revealed himself as Logos, it is the language of the New Testament and a surprising number of books in the Old Testament. As the Catechism says, “the Church has always venerated the Scriptures as she venerates the Lord’s body” (CCC 103).

              Why New Testament AND Classical Greek? #

              It is customary in most seminaries, given all the additions to the curriculum and formation over the last century, to limit instruction in the Greek language to the ability to read the New Testament in Greek. More specifically, to read the Gospels in Greek, with an eye towards future needs as a pastor to preach on the Gospel.

              The Church’s Instruction on Greek for Future Ministers #

              Towards the end of his Apostolic Constitution Veterum Sapientia, St. John XXIII elaborates on the need, in addition to a thorough grasp of Latin, for the seminarian to study Greek:

              ยง 7. Cum autem lingua Latina sit cum Graeca quam maxime coniuncta et suae conformatione naturae et scriptorum pondere antiquitus traditorum, ad eam idcirco, ut saepe numero Decessores Nostri praeceperunt, necesse est qui futuri sunt sacrorum administri iam ab inferioris et medii ordinis scholis instituantur; ut nempe, cum altioribus disciplinis operam dabunt, ac praesertim sit aut de Sacris Scripturis aut de sacra theologia academicos gradus appetent, sit ipsis facultas, non modo fontes Graecos philosophiae scholasticae, quam appellant, sed ipsos Sacrarum Scripturarum, Liturgiae, Ss. Patrum Graecorum primiformes codices adeundi probeque intellegendi.

              ยง7. Since Latin is so closely bound to Greek, both by the facts of its nature and by the importance of its ancient writers, it is necessary, as our Predecessors often taught, that future priests begin to be educated in it already in the lower and middle schools. This is so that when they come to spend time on the higher academic disciplines, especially when they seek academic degrees in Scriptural Studies or in sacred Theology, they may have direct access and the capacity to understand well not only the sources of Scholastic philosophy but also those of Holy Scripture, of Liturgy, and the early codices of the Greek Fathers.

              That is, St. John XXIII has instructed that the seminarian has four targets in his study of Greek:

              1. The Sacred Scriptures
              2. The Liturgy
              3. The Greek Fathers
              4. The Greek sources of Scholastic Philosophy

              Greek and Sacred Scripture #

              Greek gives access to the inspired text of the New Testament and some books of the Old Testament. A knowledge of Greek also gives a seminarian access to the Septuagint.

              Greek and the Liturgy #

              Greek was the foundational language of many liturgies in Church history (Antiochene, Alexandrine, etc.). In the Roman rite, besides the explicit use of the Kyrie, we should remember the Scriptural basis of much of the prayers in the liturgy.

              Greek and the Church Fathers #

              In addition to Scripture, Greek gives the seminarian access to the texts of the Apostolic and Church Fathers. Not all of those Fathers wrote in the Koine dialect of the New Testament. By covering just a few variants in spelling, morphology (forms), and orthography (spelling), the seminarian can read and enjoy Ignatius, Clement, Athanasius, Gregory Nazianzus, John Chrysostom, and many more.

              Greek and Scholastic Philosophy #

              It is safe to say that every seminarian preparing for major seminary has a lot of scholastic philosophy in his future. Much of that philosophy carries a great debt to Aristotle, Plato, and later Greek philosophers. There are forms and vocabulary essential to Greek philosophy which are not covered in the ordinary course of New Testament Greek.
